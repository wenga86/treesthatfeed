Pigeon Pea Cultivation
The cultivation of the pigeon pea goes back at least 3,000 years. Today pigeon peas are widely cultivated in all tropical and semi-tropical regions. Pigeon pea is a perennial shrub which can grow into a small tree. Producing a full crop in less than a year, this highly nutritious pea is the ideal shrub that feeds and feeds well.

Pigeon pea is an important grain legume crop of rainfed agriculture in the semi-arid tropics. The Indian subcontinent, Eastern Africa and Central America, in that order, are the world's three main pigeon pea producing regions. Pigeon pea is cultivated in more than 25 tropical and sub-tropical countries, either as a sole crop or intermixed with such cereals as sorghum, pearl millet, or maize, or with legumes, e.g. peanut. Being a legume, pigeon pea enriches soil through symbiotic nitrogen fixation.

Pigeon Pea Nutrition
Pigeon peas are both a food crop (dried peas, flour, or green vegetable peas) and a forage/cover crop. The dried peas may be sprouted briefly then cooked, for a flavor different from the green or dried peas. Sprouting also enhances the digestibility of dried pigeon peas via the reduction of indigestible sugars that would otherwise remain in the cooked dried peas. Pigeon peas are nutritionally important, as they contain high levels of protein and important amino acids. Combined with rice corn or breadfruit, pigeon peas make a well-balanced human meal.

Pigeon peas are sometimes referred to as green manure as they improve the soil with as much as 40 kg nitrogen per hectare.
