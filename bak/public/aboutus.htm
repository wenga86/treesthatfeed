About Us
Who We Are:
Trees That Feed Foundation is a not-for-profit public charity under US tax code 501(c)(3). Our organization is concerned with people in tropical countries where increases in food costs have the greatest impact. Wheat, corn and rice, which together form a large percentage of the local diet, are imported. The costs of these foods continues to rise, putting increased stress on the world's poorest people. Locally based organizations and governments are constrained by limits of their resources, while arable land lies idle in many areas. Traditionally grown field crops need more land mass while trees need much less space to grow.

Because some of us are originally from Jamaica, we have chosen to start our work there.  Paul Virtue and Mary McLaughlin grew up on farms in Jamaica and are familiar with the fruit trees they will help plant.  Now that we've established a good working model in Jamaica, we've expanded into Haiti, working with local organizations there. We have also provided trees to communities in Costa Rica and Ghana.

We believe tree crops are a large part of the answer to world food concerns. Tree crops have similar nutritional qualities to grains, yet require less input of labor, agro chemicals, fertilizers and space. Trees also restore ecological balance to land damaged by slash-and-burn farming methods, commercial logging, or neglect. Growing trees also capture and sequester carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, converting it to oxygen, and storing the carbon for decades if not centuries. Trees also create habitat for small animals and birds. Planting a tree is a winning bet.

The First Tree:
The breadfruit tree is our first choice. This South Pacific tree bears a large round fruit, larger than a grapefruit, with a green skin. The fruit is cooked and the skin is peeled for eating. When cooked, it tastes similar to unleavened bread. It can serve as a substitute for flour, rice or potatoes. We have also provided other food trees including ackee, mango, avocado, lychee, pomegranate and more.

The National Tropical Botanical Garden in Hawaii has spent years collecting and researching different varieties of breadfruit. Dr. Diane Ragone, Director of NTBG's Breadfruit Institute, is conducting extensive research on breadfruit, including identification of varieties that provide a year-round supply of fruit.
The Program:
Trees That Feed Foundation is working with Governments and not-for-profit organizations in countries with the greatest needs, including the Caribbean, Central America and Africa. We are establishing programs where selected species of food-bearing trees are introduced, increased, and distributed. Criteria for selection include:
  • Ease of growth
  • Low labor inputs
  • Early fruiting
  • Good yield
  • Nutritional qualities
  • Resistant to windstorm, pests, diseases
  • Long bearing season
  • Non-invasive
  • Either carbohydrate alternative, cash crop or base for bio-diesel
 
Trees can be grown either by small farmers, on field margins, or in urban spaces. When mature, certain trees can support growth of understory plants, including beans and coffee.

Our program will hand out seedlings and more. We hope to address issues in advance and ensure effective crops.

  • Many tropical crops produce seasonal gluts; we are looking at processing and storage for year round availability.
  • We are working with governments and local organizations to promote, expand and sustain our efforts, including education, support and distribution.
  • We are working with horticultural researchers and commercial organizations to identify breakthrough methods and keep costs low.
